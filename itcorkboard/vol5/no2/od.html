Office of the Director : Updates

The Balanced Scorecard Collaborative (www.bsccol.com) defines innovation as “The introduction ofsomething new – a new idea, method, or device. Any artifice that generates a new pattern of use, behavior, or thinking that is different from previous patterns in the same field.”

In the recent Innovation Awards organized by MIS Asia, DLSU was a finalist (though not an awardee) from 75 nominees. We earned our place in the Infrastructure category. The decision was based on our Internet infrastructure particularly the satellite dish set-up where we saved a total of P5 million from February 2000 to May 2003. Even though we did not make it, this should not stop us in introducing IT innovations to the University. This should even inspire us to strive harder and give greater positivue impact to the University.

In the nomination period, it was clear that the criteria for best innovation are based on the Balanced Scorecard (BSC) where four dimensions are to be measured and assessed to see how far each contributes to the organization’s mission, vision, and objectives. These dimensions are financial performance, customer satisfaction, efficient business processes (or internal operations), and innovation, learning and growth.

For more information about the balanced scorecard, you can visit www.balancedscorecard.com and www.bscol.com. In the first site, you can see this statement: “The Balanced Scorecard translates a company’s vision and strategy into tangible objectives and measures.” According to the Balanced Scorecard Collaborative, BSC is “a tool that translates an organization's mission and strategy into a comprehensive set of performance measures that provides the framework for a strategic measurement and management system.”

Here are some details on the BSC initiative in ITC. This serves are our guide to better plan and execute our strategies in line with the mission-vision of the campuses we serve.

Financial performance

  • Do not exceed budget.
  • Contribute to revenues and savings.
  • Percentage of projects completed on time and on budget.


Customer service

  • Client satisfaction as indicated by customer surveys completed at the end of a service request, project, and annual survey.
  • Meeting or exceeding performance objectives.


Efficient business processes

  • Uptime of IT infrastructure – i.e., DLSUnet
    (servers, desktops, peripherals, network, and internet infrastructure).
  • Uptime of IS infrastructure.
  • Process improvement.
  • Implementation of annual work plan.
  • Improvement and documentation of processes.


Innovation, learning and growth

  • Implementation of annual staff development plan.
  • Involvement of “R&D” initiatives of each team member.
  • Improvement and implementation of performance evaluation system.

One interesting and valid question that I got a few times is: “How can we contribute to revenues and savings?” Here are some examples that focus on cost whenever infrastructure is “down”. The figures may not be accurate and the formulas are not based on any scientific research.

A department has 10 faculty members. They have an average hourly rate of P200. If that department has five computers, only five faculty members could use the computer at a time. If all of them need to use the computers, five could not be productive. That results to an hourly cost of P200 x 5 = P1,000. If one of the five computers is out of order, that results to an hourly cost of P200 x 6 = P1,200. If we are only to compute the productivity cost of one faculty member on one computer, the cost would be P200 x 8 = P1,600 per eight -hour day if the computer is not fixed within one day.

Let’s assume that the University has an average hourly rate of P150 per faculty member. If there are 10 computers which are out of order and are fixed only after one day, the productivity cost for an eight-hour day would be P150 x 10 x 8 = P12,000. For a week, this translates to P12,000 x 7 = P84,000.


If our e-mail server is not available, “everyone” won’t be able to communicate via e-mail. Assume 50 people (with an average hourly rate of P200) need to urgently communicate within the hour, this can computed as P200 x 50 = P10,000 per hour.

The above examples give straightforward cost computations. Let’s consider the other side of the coin but more difficult to quantify. Let’s assume we are able to make on-line enrollment available – i.e., zero problem. This includes the Internet infrastructure plus our program, servers, and network. What should we consider?

People from the Registrar’s Office need not bother about the manual enrollment process. Assume eight-hour enrollment period per day for four days and hourly rate of P85 for six people. This will be P85 x 6 x 8 x 4 = P16,320. For three trimesters, that will be about P48,960. Let’s just round this off to P49,000 per year.


Assuming automated course approval but this is fully implemented already, we can include savings from Academic Assistants, who do academic advising for students. For simplicity, let’s also assume that the savings would be equal to that on Registrar’s Office personnel. That’ll be around P49,000 per year.


People from ITC need not set-up computers and network cables at the Conservatory. Install and uninstall time would be about five hours (example only) for four people. Assume hourly rate is P100. The savings would be P100 x 2 x 5 = P1,000 per enrollment period. For three trimesters, that will be about P3,000 per school year.

So far we have P49,000 + P49,000 + P3,000 = P101,000 per year. But I think the actual figure is higher. The counterpart of this savings is the cost of developing and maintaining the programs and licenses. The license for the database would be the same. IT infrastructure could remain the same – i.e., using the same servers, network, and PCs…actually, lesser DLSU PCs. Here are some more items to consider.

Students are able to enroll in less than 10 minutes, assuming their accounts are okay. No queuing, except for payment (for now). Let’s assume that they’re happy and satisfied. What figure do we put on customer satisfaction?


On-line enrollment adds to the prestige and reputation of DLSU. What figure do we put on this?

Another way to contribute to savings and revenue is simply by doing what we’re expected to do. I got feedback that one applicant chose the blue school because he/she thought does not have wireless network infrastructure. Assuming this really happened, we needed to promote our wireless services some more. I’ve informed MCO about this. Let’s hope that all IT initiatives and facilities would be made known in the usual marketing channels (e.g., student catalog, DLSU/Library/Admissions/DLSAA web sites, conference web sites that we host, student publications, local dailies, vendor publications, foreign magazines, professional organizations, and virtual communities). By helping improve the prestige and reputation of DLSU, we also help attract applicants, scholars, researchers, employees, and donors for DLSU. And for those who are already here, we also help improve their/our sense of pride and fulfillment in being part of the DLSU community.

Another unchartered territory is vendor resources. One day, HP Philippines asked me what else it can do for DLSU to improve its services and the “partnership”. I gave my inputs and also asked if HP is interested in financing a scholarship program. It first started at P150,000 per school year. But when HP asked for more details (e.g., background of scholarship and the degree program concerned), I passed the ball to CCS since CCS is in a better position to deal with this. The scholarship requirement became around P1 million. I think this is the scholarship program requirement of DLSU to make it viable and sustainable. Whether or not this HP scholarship program pushes through, we/ITC did something to contribute to revenues and savings. I wonder when Microsoft will ask us about their services. It might be a good time to e-mail them.
