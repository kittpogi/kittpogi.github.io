Our Challenge: Taking Control of Intervening Projects

by: Samuel P. Mallare

It’s the planning season once again. We’re reviewing our performance and projects. We’re also coming up with plans for next school year. I enjoy planning sessions (and I hope you do, too) because we get to review our current achievements and problems. We also get to have a preview of our future achievements, which are embodied in our plans. The simple assessment of how good our plans were made last school year is the quantity and quality of achievements and problems we have this school year.

We often come across the term “intervening activities.” In the past decade, there was not a single year that this was not identified as a problem source. “We have too many intervening activities.” Yeah, right! But is it a problem or part of reality that we need to manage? Or, is that the real problem? Intervening activities are treated as interruptions to our routines. They intervene with our rhythmic task cycles. Maybe we can ask ourselves, what are these “intervening” activities? How much time do they take to complete? How many resources do they use? Are they really “intervening”? What can we do to improve the situation?

It was reported before that one cause of intervening requests from our customers is poor planning on the part of our customers. Another is that our customers do not know what they need or they keep changing their minds. Let’s assume that these are all true. Hold that thought for a moment.

In an organization, “project managers” were asked to identify the activities one can do in a year in terms of man-hours. Each team member has the same number of man-hours in a year. Let’s try to compute that.

40 hours per week x 52 weeks in a year = 2,080 hours

Let’s deduct all leaves and non-working days.
18-day vacation leave
15-day sick leave
5-day emergency leave
9-day Christmas break
15 non-working days (estimate number of holidays)
5 days due to class/work suspension
…excluding service leaves, leaves without pay, and deloading for graduate studies

This totals to 67 days or about 70 days.
70 days x 8 hours per day = 560 hours
2,080 hours – 560 hours =1,520 hours or 190 working days.

Wow, that’s 190 working days out of 365 days in a year! Let’s make that 200 days. 200 working days is around nine months if there are 22 working days in a month. Of course, not everyone will use up all the leaves. I do hope that very few will be sick each year. Some holidays will fall on weekends. There are other possibilities that an anticipated non-working day would be a working day. Let’s consider all these as our slack time. What should we do with 200 working days per year to achieve our vision?

Let’s go back to the intervening projects. A project manager’s horror story is for his/her projects to go out of control. Having so many intervening projects is, in a sense, going out of control. How do we optimize our man-hours while reducing intervening tasks? How many projects can we handle? How much flexibility can we provide ourselves for planned and intervening projects? Which of the intervening projects could be part of planned/anticipated projects or could be routines?

Here are more questions. What can we gain from reviewing past intervening projects? What can we gain from meeting with our customers and reviewing their requirements with them? I think the more intervening projects we have, the more we should be meeting with our customers more frequently. This is to know their new requirements, changes in their processes, and other points of improvement we can have in terms of process and automation.

It’s a matter of identifying and maybe even anticipating their needs. Then we can integrate them into our plans – project plans, HR hiring and development plans, and infrastructure improvement plans. [continue...]
