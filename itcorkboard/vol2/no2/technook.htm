Draw flowcharts with Word 2000 and PowerPoint 2000

You need to draw a diagram, such as a flowchart, but you're not sure if you should use Word 2000 or PowerPoint® 2000. Word is a word-processing program that includes drawing tools, whereas PowerPoint is geared towards slide presentations and drawing. Both are easy to use, but which is better for you? Let's see how Bonn and Kitty draw a flowchart with each program.

Draw with Word 2000

Bonn chooses Word to create his flowchart. On the View menu, he points to Toolbars and clicks Drawing. On the Drawing toolbar, he then clicks AutoShapes and points to Flowchart to see appropriate shapes. To get a description of a particular shape, he pauses on it. 

 

 

Insert shapes  To insert a shape (for example, the Flowchart: Process shape), Bonn simply clicks it, positions the pointer where he wants the shape inserted, and clicks again. Because he wants text included in the shape, he right-clicks it, selects Add Text, and starts typing. If the text is too long or the font size too big for the shape, he can either resize the shape by clicking it and dragging the sizing handles to make it larger, or make the font smaller by selecting the text and clicking a smaller point size in the Font Size box.

 

 

Connect shapes  To connect shapes, Bonn just clicks the Arrow button on the Drawing toolbar, clicks a border on the first shape, holds down the mouse button, and releases it where he wants the arrowhead. To add text to an arrow, Bonn clicks the Text Box button on the Drawing toolbar and positions the text box alongside the arrow. Because he does not want the text box to have visible borders, he selects the box, and then on the Format menu, selects Text Box. On the Colors and Lines tab, under Line, in the Color drop-down, he selects No Line.

 

 

 

 

Group shapes  To group the shapes in his flowchart so that they stay and move together, Bonn selects the objects he wants included by holding down SHIFT as he clicks each one. On the Drawing toolbar, he clicks Draw, and then clicks Group.

 

Note  If you resize the grouped objects with the sizing handles, you may need to adjust the font size of the text.

Unique features  Word also allows Bonn to wrap text around shapes. He simply makes sure that the shape is not grouped and selects it. Then on the Format menu, he clicks the command for the type of object selected - in his case, AutoShape - and clicks the Layout tab. He chooses the wrapping style that he wants to apply.

 

Draw with PowerPoint 2000

To draw her flowchart, Kitty chooses PowerPoint. She right-clicks any toolbar and selects the Drawing toolbar. Like Bonn, Kitty clicks AutoShapes and points to Flowchart to see appropriate shapes. She also pauses on a shape to see its description.

Insert shapes  To insert a shape, she clicks it, positions the pointer where she wants the shape inserted, and clicks again. Unlike in Word, the shape has a colored fill in PowerPoint. To change this fill, Kitty first selects the object. Then on the Format menu, she clicks AutoShape and the Colors and Lines tab. Under Fill, in the Color drop-down box, she selects a new color.

Connect shapes  To connect shapes, like Bonn, Kitty just clicks the Arrow button on the Drawing toolbar, clicks a border on the first shape, holds down the mouse button, and releases it where she wants the arrowhead. Adding text to an arrow in PowerPoint is a bit easier, however. She simply clicks the Text Box button on the Drawing toolbar, and then clicks the place on the page where she wants to insert the text.

 

 

Group shapes  To group the shapes, Kitty selects the objects by holding down SHIFT as she clicks each one. On the Drawing toolbar, she clicks Draw, and then clicks Group.

Note  If you resize the grouped objects with the sizing handles, you may need to adjust the font size of the text.

 

 

Unique features  PowerPoint also offers some features that Word does not. To insert text, for example, PowerPoint allows Kitty to click inside the shape and type. If her text does not fit inside the object, she just selects the object. Then on the Format menu, she clicks AutoShape, clicks the Text Box tab, and selects the Word wrap text in AutoShape check box to wrap the text to the width of the object.

 

 

In addition, she can resize the shape by clicking it and dragging the sizing handles. If she wants the size of the shape to remain the same, however, she can make the text smaller by selecting the text and clicking a smaller point size in the Font Size box. With the Word wrap text in AutoShape check box selected, the width of the object will not change when she resizes the shape.

 

 

Kitty can also select the Resize AutoShape to fit text check box. With the Resize AutoShape to fit text check box selected, the shape will not resize until she changes the font size.
