Office of the Director : Updates

Financial Perspective of the Balanced ScorUecard (BSC)
By: Samuel P. Mallare

When I presented this to some people in ITC, one question raised was how we could perform as far as the financial perspective is concerned. There was no problem regarding the other BSC perspective – i.e., customer satisfaction, internal processes, and organizational learning – because they were pretty much self-explanatory. We are seen as a cost center. We spend money. We do not teach students. As you know, the student body is the revenue source of the University.

The challenge of contributing to the financial performance of the University is somehow difficult to measure. Perhaps this is because we do not actually measure this activity even though we have done several things to save money – i.e., doing more with less. One project that we measured was the satellite dishes used for Internet access. Over a period of 3.25 years, we saved a total of P5 million plus recovery of purchase cost of two satellite dishes, which amounted to P1.2 million. That’s a total of P6.2 million.

Our automated information systems have enabled the University to save as well. This covers our programs, electronic records, web sites, and process improvement projects. We only have not measured this yet. It is also tedious to measure. Our programs have improved the productivity of people. They have helped administrators in making decisions faster as a result of faster information retrieval. They have enabled the University to re-assign some people to perform other productive work. They have improved the image of the University as a technology-enabled organization. Last but not least, they have helped the University satisfy its customers. Now, what number can represent the value in all these?

This school year, we again have the opportunity to help the University save money. This is with the use of a software called BeTwin. It enables one complete set of computer to function as two up to five units. We only need to connect a “twin” set – i.e., a monitor, a keyboard, a mouse, and speakers via a USB hub. For every deployment of BeTwin, we can save about P18,000. Using this figure, we can save P1 million when we deploy 56 units of BeTwin. Assuming my computations are slightly erratic and the savings is only P15,000 per deployment. We can save P1 million when we deploy 66 units. If we annualize the savings, it is about P9,000 per deployment per year. Using this figure, we can save P1 million per year if we have 111 deployments. I’m confident that we can achieve this. We will even exceed this amount in the future with the help of BeTwin.

I encourage everyone to be more conscious of financial implications of our projects. I’m sure that these cost savings will also give you fulfillment in your work. As someone once told me, more points in heaven.

IT Security
By: Samuel P. Mallare

The Office of the Director is still focused on improving our IT security as well as PC maintenance service. IT security is a primary weakness of ITC while the PC maintenance service is something faculty members have been complaining about.

On IT security, we have been making good progress over the past several months especially on the use of SSL and IPsec and several server and network management initiatives. Our next move is to implement SSL on MS Outlook 2003 client with Exchange 2003. This will not only improve IT security but also the productivity of our customers. As you know, MS Outlook client has more functionalities than its web-based counterpart, OWA. CFO is currently working on some tools to improve our software patch management capability.

On PC maintenance, there were issues raised in the Council of Deans and Faculty Studies Committee. These were mainly from the College of Engineering although there were perceptions that CCS and COS also complained. All these were clarified already. CCS and COS had no major complaints with our services. As for COE, the Director had a meeting with five people from COE representing various COE departments. Again, there were no major issues raised regarding our services except for three items related to V103 computer lab. These were either already resolved or minor problems. The Faculty Studies Committee has not given a detailed report on their complaint. This means that their complaints were based merely on perceptions rather than real issues. Let’s hope that this is the start of a good perception/image regarding our PC maintenance services.

I would like to thank and congratulate CFO on the various improvements. We have more things to do but I’m sure we’re already on the right track. Just remember and do what PRIO teaches – PDCA (Plan-Do-Check-Act).

Back to IT security, our students, who have problems enabling their MLS accounts, e-mail our Help Desk with all details needed (e.g., OR number) and sometimes even with their passwords. This is dangerous because if there were a hacker/cracker sniffing the packets of our network or if our e-mail system were compromised, they could get hold of the passwords and/or details needed to get the passwords.


I recently read a short article from W2Knews that a server of UC Berkeley got compromised. “A computer hacker accessed names and Social Security numbers of about 1.4 million Californians after breaking into a University of California, Berkeley, computer system in possibly the worst attack of its kind ever suffered by the school. You need to scan for vulnerabilities the moment they come out, and patch 'em like greased lightning.”


We need to research to continue improving the security and quality of our automated information systems and databases. In terms of processes, I hope that we’ll be able to implement the system code and system documentation repository procedures. I’m sure everyone will pitch in.
