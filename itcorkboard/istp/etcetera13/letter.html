UAAP : Letter from a concerned Atenean


To whom it may concern:


As an alumnus of the Ateneo (GS'90 HS'94 AB MEco '98), a member of the Guidon sports staff in 1996-97 and a recipient of the Ateneo High School's Distinguished Individual Achievement Award for Sports in 1992, I would like to express my utter disgust towards the actions of the Blue Babble Battalion member who led the prayer before Game One of the Season 65


UAAP Men's Basketball Finals. I don't recall the exact words, but he declared before the Araneta Coliseum audience and millions of television viewers that "we know it's our time" and invoked God's blessing as "we claim our victory". He implied, then, that Ateneans have a divine right to glory and triumph. His one statement of pride and hubris has undermined whatever moral high ground we had left in this basketball championship. It has tainted my respect for a University-wide effort, which has, as University President Fr. Ben Nebres claimed in his statement last Monday, "shown us teamwork, faith, character and courage-the ultimate reasons we believe in the importance of varsity sports in Ateneo life." His one statement has also given perspective and clarity to many images that I see when watching Ateneo basketball games, where distinguished" alumni jab their middle fingers at the DLSU bench, rabid fathers shout cuss words while their wives and children listen on, and University officials have recently learned how to pressure teachers to suspend classes to support one varsity team among dozens of others.


With this in mind, I am considering returning the Distinguished Individual Achievement Award in Sports award I was given ten years ago. It brings me sadness to look at it as it no longer serves as the banner of an institution that carries itself with humility-a key virtue which St. Ignatius extols in his Spiritual Exercises. I recall the awards ceremony when the late Fr. Jose Cruz narrated one of his favorite World War II stories about a bunch of Ateneans who took to the battlefield against Japanese invaders-and died trying. He told us that sports was a similar kind of service, and that our every action was a prayer meant to be exercised with dignity and honor. He also emphasized the Athlete's Prayer, which tells us to ask for nothing but a field that is fair, and the dignity to salute our foes in victory or defeat. We won the game today, but our behavior cost us something more deeply rooted in the Ateneo ideal-our honor. The Ateneo that I see today is but a shadow of its former self-"full of sound and fury, signifying nothing."


Very truly yours,

Michael Palacios
26 September 2002
