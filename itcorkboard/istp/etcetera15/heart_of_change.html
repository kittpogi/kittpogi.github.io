Getting to the Heart of Change
by: Dan Cohen
Contributed by: Samuel P. Mallare

Changing hearts, as well as minds, is key to managing change around IT implementations.

Change is both our biggest challenge and our biggest ally in creating a better future. This is especially so in the area of IT. Technology adoption is fundamentally about change: new systems require new processes, new thinking and new ways of doing things. Yet, most leaders, IT or otherwise, do not lead or manage large-scale change well. As a result, many organisations fail to realise the full benefits of their implementations.

The single biggest challenge in any transformation is not culture, strategy or systems—but getting people to change their behaviour. We discovered a flow to successful change: eight fundamental steps that must be undertaken—usually, but not necessarily, in succession—in order to create a climate for change and to engage the whole organisation in supporting the transformation. In the most successful change efforts, people use the see-feel-change method to achieve behavioural change within each of these eight steps:

Step 1: Increase Urgency

Those who are most successful at significant change—whether CIOs at the top or small work groups at the bottom—begin by creating a sense of urgency among relevant people. Urgency is an energising force; it is what gets people on their toes and ready to move. Although logic, data and analysis are important for establishing why the organisation should change (and why management should invest large sums of money in the initiative), it does not have enough power to overcome the three primary feelings that undermine change: complacency, fear and anger.

That's why successful change leaders frequently use compelling, eye-catching, dramatic situations to show people what the problems are and to create an urgent, emotionally charged need to resolve them. Visualisations provide useful ideas that hit people at a deeper level than surface thinking. They evoke a visceral response that reduces emotions that block change (complacency, fear and anger) and enhance those that support it (urgency, optimism and faith).

Step 2: Build the Guiding Team

With urgency up, the more successful change leaders pull together a guiding team. This means assembling a group with the right characteristics — those with the authority to make required decisions and sufficient influence to garner essential resources. For instance, guiding team members in an IT implementation could range from the CIO to an influential end user to a savvy procurement manager.

Step 3: Get the Vision Right

In the most effective instances of large-scale change, the guiding team creates a clear and practical direction for the transformation. This means answering questions such as: What change is needed? What is our vision of the new organisation? What should not be altered? How is the best way to make the vision a reality? Far too often, guiding teams either set no clear direction or embrace visions that are not sensible.

Step 4: Communicate for Buy-In

In successful change efforts, the leaders communicate the vision and strategies in simple, candid and heartfelt ways. Also, they communicate widely, ensuring that all levels of the organisation that are going to play a role in making the change happen are reached by a consistent message. After all, the ultimate goal is to get as many people as possible acting to make the vision a reality.

Many IT leaders under-communicate or communicate poorly without even knowing it. This is because too many people fail to see that good communication is not just data transfer. You need to show people something that addresses their anxieties, accepts their anger, that is credible in a very gut-level sense, and that evokes faith in the vision.

Step 5: Empower Action

In the best scenarios, change leaders empower people to take action. In this instance "empower" does not mean "giving power" and then walking away. Instead it means helping people to carry out the vision by removing obstacles — whether it's a disempowering boss, inadequate information and information systems or self-confidence barriers in people's minds. IT implementations often fail due to one of the most common emotions found in the business world today: frustration. When barriers are not removed on a timely basis, people often become so frustrated in their attempts to do things right that they give up. As a result, change is undermined.

Step 6: Create Short-Term Wins

In cases of great success, empowered people are helped to produce highly visible short-term wins. These well-publicised victories are critical to maintaining momentum in IT implementations because they reward hard work, produce a feeling of accomplishment and promote a sense of optimism. They also generate strong visible proof that the change is moving forward, which lays the groundwork for larger behavioural changes. Without incorporating quick, visible, short-term wins into their project plans, organisations run the risk of sinking their change efforts, as the cynics and sceptics take over the ship.

Step 7: Don't Let Up

As the old adage goes, "It ain't over 'til it's over." In the best cases, change leaders don't let up; they continue with wave after wave of change until a complete transformation vision becomes a reality. After the first set of short-term wins, a change initiative should have direction and momentum. From there, effective leaders use this momentum to broaden the change effort. This means tackling the bigger and deeper changes to the organisation, such as realigning processes, technology and systems that will deliver the real benefits.

Step 8: Making Change Stick

In the most successful change efforts, leaders throughout the organisation make change stick by anchoring it in a supportive culture. Many efforts fail, however, because leaders try to instantly create a new culture, but this is not how organisational dynamics work. A culture truly changes only when a new way of operating has been shown to succeed over some minimum period of time. To make change last, the new behaviour must be nurtured so that it becomes "the way things are done" in the organisation. In other words, norms of behaviour and shared values need to be reinforced and made part of both formal and informal systems. Then, slowly, a new organisational culture will take root. Without anchoring the change efforts in this new, supportive culture, the organisation runs the risk of sliding back into old habits and traditions with remarkable speed.

 

Dan Cohen is principal, Deloitte Consulting, and co-author of "Heart of Change" (Harvard Business School Press, 2002 ).
