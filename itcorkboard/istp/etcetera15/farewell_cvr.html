A Reflection on Life
by: Celina Rivera

It all started when my husband, after receiving his approval of Australian citizenship, decided to leave for Australia to look for a job. Prior to this, his self-started software development firm folded up and he only had a handful of clients. Unfortunately, he was not that lucky to land a fulltime job after that. With kids fast growing up and education getting costlier, his decision was timely. At first, his life in Australia was not that easy, coping with a bout of homesickness, and getting odd jobs while waiting for a fulltime job. After two months, he did land one but he still had that desire to be with this family. Frankly, I was not comfortable with his decision to leave because I knew it would rock my peaceful life. I would be a mom and dad to my kids while still holding on to my job; and there was the decision that I too have to make when he does get us.

The last February, he came home bent on convincing me to file our family’s migration application. I spent sleepless nights thinking about what I have to give up – my career at DLSU, our house and other properties, the company of my relatives and friends, having a househelp, this country, and worse eventually my citizenship. I never really savored the thought of giving up my being a Filipino (how nationalistic!) But we prayed about it and thought if it’s not really God’s will, then this application will not progress. And my husband wanted me to explore this option by trying it out there before closing my door to this opportunity. So we agree that if I don’t like it there, or it would not be to our advantage to stay there for good, we’ll be coming back here.

Since then, I felt that my life was on a string, full of uncertainty. It’s as if you’re pregnant, you know that you’re about to give birth soon but don’t know when and you’re afraid of what will happen. Even the children’s enrolment was delayed because I wasn’t sure when the visa will be approved. Then last June 19, the inevitable came. We got the notice of the approval, three months after filing, and we had to be there by October. Not feeling very sure if it was really true, I went to the embassy to submit some forms and our passports. After about thirty minutes, my number was called and our passports were stamped!

I didn’t know if I would be happy or sad. Some would be ecstatic or excited because processing time was short (the maximum waiting time was nine months). My children were not that happy, too because they were just starting the school year and gaining new friends. I, too, was not very glad because I have to start thinking of arrangements for our house’s rental, disposal of our things, notification of my kids’ schools and request for transfer documents, packing up, finishing work in the office and turning over work to my “replacement”, etc.

But I had to make a choice, between career and family, and for me, the latter came first. I wouldn’t want a “broken relationship” or much more, a “broken home”. Still I was having second thoughts even on the decision on our flight schedule. But God made me realize that it’s not really the date that mattered now but the feeling of not going because of too many attachments in my life. I guess if the objective of a decision is good, it will be God’s will. And God wants me to trust in Him, to give up these attachments, to risk everything, and to turn over to His hands the string that controls my life.

With my decision, I will miss DLSU where I’ve learned a lot and spent most of my waking hours from Monday to Friday for the last fifteen years. I will miss my friends whom I’ve gained through the years. I will miss the web development team whose creative passion and resourcefulness inspired me to lead them to produce outputs better than the last one it has done. I will miss the DAO people whom I enjoy discussing more technical stuff. I will miss the volleyball games, my once a year workout in the sports complex, and of course winning it. I will miss the ITC, (TGIH/F, the outings, Christmas party, etc.) where I’ve spent the best years of my life. One year of absence is quite long for me.

With a new country, culture, job and family conditions to face, I can see another chapter in my life unfolding before me. I have only the faith that God will always be there for us. I am not saying goodbye to the previous chapters. I am just putting a bookmark….so I can come back again in the future.


An addendum….
(13 Oct 2003)

It was drizzling on the morning of 27 Sept 2003 and the temperature was about 10 degrees Celsius. It was not a warm welcome after all! It was nice to breath in clean air but we felt the chill on our noses. We were met by a couple friend and we felt the icy cold climate despite our thick jackets. In the house we rented, we huddled in front of the heater. The kids said the airconditioning unit was on, 24 hours a day, in and out of the house.

So far, I find the people friendly, the transport system is so organized (bus/train/trams stops have exact schedules), food is cheap in proportion to the income, and schools accept children even in the last quarter of the school year. But the “walking distance” is far (a fifteen minute walk to the shops and market), shops close earlier than our malls in the Philippines and the kids find the nightlife very quiet (you don’t see people walking around the city when it gets dark, maybe because it’s too cold and they have no reason to hang out of their houses).

In a short time, I will be adjusting to the demands of this new life. I’ll be filling out another chapter and I hope I get enough experience to put in. Till next issue…..
