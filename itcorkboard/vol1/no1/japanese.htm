Learning the Japanese way...

SEIRI (Sort) Separate the necessary from the unnecessary. Discard the unnecessary.

Practicals:

  • Take a look at your table.
  • Make a list of things that you can see on it.
  • Label each items by frequency of use.
  • Make a clear distinction of which materials and tools are necessary in the workplace.
  • After identifying the things that you frequently use, look for a place where you can put the other items that are not frequently used.
  • Arrange things so that they can be understood or found at a glance.

SEITON (Systematize) There is a big gap between limited resources to unlimited wants. So, conserve time and energy, focus on the important, not just the urgent.

Practicals:

  • Take a blank sheet of paper, and divide it into three columns, which you label, "Must do", "should do" and "Can do".
  • Under the "Must do" heading make a list of everything that has to be done, without fail.
  • In the list of "Should do" items, put tasks of secondary importance, perhaps items that needs to be done next week but aren’t really urgent or pressing.
  • The "can do" list includes items which will eventually need your attention, and as time passes those items may move from right to left as they gain importance.
  • Number the items, listing the most important one first, the next important as number two and so forth.
  • The number one item is the "Must do" list. Though you are tempted to avoid that phone call because you don’t especially want to talk to that person, don’t put it off.
  • Remember: Do the most important one first, and don’t go on to the next item until you have finished that task.

SEISO (Sweep) Make immediate repairs and constant checking and cleaning

Practicals:

  • Examine your work area from corner to corner and from wall to wall.
  • Eliminate all garbage and non-essential items.
  • Make some sort of evaluation criteria for straightening up.
  • Do the cleaning and evaluation weekly.
  • Remember: Litters come in many varieties, any of which should be just as offensive as cigarette butts; items that haven’t been used for months or even years, obsolete documents, machines that is not current being use and similar items.

SEIKETSU (Sanitize) Make time for preventive maintenance. Safety first with a clean environment.

Practicals:

  • Avoid unsafe acts and eliminate unsafe conditions
  • Observe personal hygiene
  • Prevent your workplace from getting contaminated
  • Eliminate impurities in the air: dust, trash, smoke and dirt.
  • Improve on anything that is offensive to the five senses.
  • Remember: Cleanliness is next to godliness.

SHITSUKE (Self-Discipline) Change is the only constant thing in this world. And it takes a firm decision to change for the better. Habits are learned and bad habits are unlearned.

Practicals:

  • Evaluate yourself
  • Weigh everything that you want to do and what you can actually do.
  • Differentiate things you can change versus things you can’t change.
  • Reflect on the things that you ought to change.
  • Make a firm decision to change for the better
  • Stand on your decision.
  • Remember: Actions speak louder than words

If the Japanese could do it, we can definitely do it also. All we need is a firm decision to change and a discipline to continue. 

References:

  1. Coping with Stress by Harold Sala
  2. Putting 5S to Work by Hiroyuki Hirano
