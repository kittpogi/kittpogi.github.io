    The Japanese Way
 

Learning the Japanese Way part 2

Care for a follow up on the S’s of Japan? Here are additional tips from Japan to further spice up our quest for quality… Do you know that apart from the Five S, there is such a framework called the Seven S?

Anyway, Seven S represents a simple but powerful insight into what makes an organization succeed. Life is complex and ever changing. Everything is changing every second… 

And the only constant thing in this world is change.

7’s is not just concerned with the house keeping rather it aims to target the key factors affecting the organization’s ability to implement new strategies. The factors are to be taken as a whole, neglecting any one factor can adversely affect successful change.

 

The Seven S are:

Strategy.
Plan or course of action leading to the allocation of an organization’s scarce resources, over time, to reach identified goals. Come up with innovative strategies giving emphasis to quality.

Structure.
Characterization of the organization chart (i.e., functional, decentralized, etc.) Make temporary structural changes to cope with specific strategic tasks without abandoning basic structural divisions throughout the organization.

Systems.
Proceduralized reports and routinized processes such as meeting formats. Come up with IT based on-line access.

Staff.
"Demographic" description of important personnel categories within the firm (i.e., engineers, entrepreneurs, mba’s etc.) staff is not meant in line terms. Organization should view people as resources to be managed aggressively- that is to be nurtured, developed, guarded and allocated.

Practicals: Assign mentors to talented individuals, put them into fast track programs, expose to top management and rapidly move into positions of real responsibility.

Style.
Characterization of how key managers behave in achieving the organization’s goals; also the cultural style of the organization.

Practicals: Key Managers should change their perception of "gemba" (Japanese term for where the customers come into contact with the services being offered). They should regard it as one of the most important place in the organization. They should not be just sitting on their desk and giving orders based on reports received via mail, email, phone or fax.

Skills.
Distinctive capabilities of key personnel or the firm as a whole.

Practicals: Highlight activities, talents, and skills that your organization can do best and for which you are known.

Superordinate Goals.
These are not formally stated organizational objectives but rather guiding concepts, values and aspirations that unite an organization in some common purpose.

Practicals: Come up with a slogan that will best fit your organization’s ojectives. e.g "Customer Service" by IBM and "New Products" by 3M.

 

 

References:
1. The Art of Japanese Management by Richard T. Pascale and Anthony G. Athos
2. Management by James A.F. Stoner and Edward Freeman
3. About Gemba by Central Magazine
